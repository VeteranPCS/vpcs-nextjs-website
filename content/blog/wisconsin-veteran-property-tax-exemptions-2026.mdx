If you serve in the military or have served, Wisconsin offers one of the most generous property tax breaks in the country for fully disabled veterans, paid back through the state income tax return. Wisconsin is home to Fort McCoy, the Army's total-force training center near Sparta, and Truax Field, the Air National Guard base in Madison, so it draws many military families after a PCS, which is short for Permanent Change of Station, the official military move from one duty station to another. Wisconsin handles its benefit differently from most states: rather than lowering your home's assessed value, it lets a qualifying veteran claim back the property taxes paid. This guide explains how that works in plain language and points you to the state's own sources. Tax law changes, so treat these figures as a starting point and confirm the current rules before you file.

## How Wisconsin Helps Disabled Veterans With Property Taxes

Wisconsin does not exempt a disabled veteran's home from assessment. Instead, it gives a refundable credit equal to the property taxes paid. The U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, known as the VA, is the federal agency that rates service-connected disabilities, and the top rating is what unlocks this benefit.

As the [Wisconsin Department of Veterans Affairs explains](https://dva.wi.gov/services/housing-and-financial-services/property-tax-credit/), the Veterans and Surviving Spouses Property Tax Credit is a credit for the property taxes paid on a veteran's or surviving spouse's primary residence and up to one acre of land. Because the credit is refundable, if it is larger than the income tax you owe, the difference comes back to you as a refund.

### Who Qualifies

To qualify, a veteran must have a service-connected disability rating of 100 percent, or be rated 100 percent based on individual unemployability, as confirmed by the VA. Wisconsin also expects the veteran to have been a state resident when entering active duty or to have lived in Wisconsin for a five-year period after entering service. The Wisconsin Department of Veterans Affairs certifies eligibility, and the Department of Revenue then administers the credit through the state income tax return.

### How Much the Credit Is Worth

The credit covers 100 percent of the property taxes paid on the principal residence, including up to one acre of land. As the Department of Revenue's [guidance on claiming the credit](https://www.revenue.wi.gov/Pages/FAQS/ise-vetclaim.aspx) describes, you claim it on your Wisconsin income tax return for the year the property taxes are paid. For a fully disabled veteran who owns and lives in the home, this can offset the entire property tax bill on the residence.

### Surviving Spouses

The benefit can carry to a surviving spouse. An unremarried surviving spouse of an eligible veteran, or of a service member who died while on active duty, may claim the credit. Confirm the conditions with the Wisconsin Department of Veterans Affairs, which issues the certification.

## How It Works With Your Local Tax Bill

Property tax in Wisconsin is assessed and collected at the local level by your municipality and county, and you still pay that bill when it comes due. The relief arrives later: you claim the Veterans and Surviving Spouses Property Tax Credit on your Wisconsin income tax return, and because the credit is refundable, you can get the full amount back even if it is more than your income tax.

The first step is the certification from the Wisconsin Department of Veterans Affairs, which verifies your rating and eligibility; you then attach that certification when you claim the credit. ## Military Pay and Wisconsin State Income Tax

Because this benefit runs through the income tax return, it helps to know how Wisconsin treats military income. Wisconsin has a graduated income tax, but it shields military pay broadly. The way the state handles your pay depends on the type of pay, with a strong result for most military families.

Military retirement pay is fully exempt. As the state's [military income guidance](https://www.revenue.wi.gov/Pages/FAQS/pcs-military.aspx) explains, all retirement payments from the U.S. military retirement system, including Survivor Benefit Plan payments, are exempt from Wisconsin income tax, with no age or income limit. For active-duty pay, Wisconsin allows a subtraction of basic, special, and incentive pay received for active duty, and military pay that is exempt for federal purposes, such as combat pay, is exempt for Wisconsin too. VA disability compensation is not part of your federal income, so Wisconsin does not tax it either. Between exempt retirement pay, the active-duty subtraction, and untaxed VA compensation, many military families owe little or no Wisconsin income tax on their service income. Because rates and rules can change, confirm the current details with the Department of Revenue before you file.

## A Note for Military Spouses: MSRRA

If you are a military spouse, the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act, known as MSRRA, may matter to you. MSRRA is a federal law that lets a military spouse keep a home state for tax and voting purposes even after moving on military orders. You do not automatically become a Wisconsin resident just because your service member got orders here.

Under the related federal rules, a service member, the spouse, or both may choose the service member's home state, the spouse's home state, or the service member's permanent duty station for residency. Because that choice affects both states' taxes, confirm yours before you file. For the property tax credit in this guide, what usually matters most is that the qualifying veteran owns and lives in the home as the primary residence.

## How to Apply and Where to Verify

First, get certified by the [Wisconsin Department of Veterans Affairs](https://dva.wi.gov/services/housing-and-financial-services/property-tax-credit/), which confirms your 100 percent service-connected or individual-unemployability rating and your residency. Then claim the credit on your Wisconsin income tax return, following the Department of Revenue's [instructions for claiming the credit](https://www.revenue.wi.gov/Pages/FAQS/ise-vetclaim.aspx). Because the rules can change, confirm the current details with both agencies before you file.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do disabled veterans pay property taxes in Wisconsin?

In practice, a fully disabled veteran can get them back. You still pay the local property tax bill, then claim the Veterans and Surviving Spouses Property Tax Credit on your state income tax return for 100 percent of the taxes paid on your primary residence and up to one acre. Because the credit is refundable, the full amount can come back even if it exceeds your income tax. Confirm your eligibility with the Wisconsin Department of Veterans Affairs.

### What disability rating do I need for the Wisconsin veteran credit?

You need a VA service-connected disability rating of 100 percent, or a 100 percent rating based on individual unemployability. The Wisconsin Department of Veterans Affairs certifies eligibility before you claim the credit.

### Does Wisconsin tax military retirement pay?

No. All retirement payments from the U.S. military retirement system, including Survivor Benefit Plan payments, are exempt from Wisconsin income tax, with no age or income limit. Active-duty basic, special, and incentive pay can be subtracted, and VA disability compensation is not taxed.

### How do I claim the Wisconsin veterans property tax credit?

First, get certified by the Wisconsin Department of Veterans Affairs. Then claim the credit on your Wisconsin income tax return for the year you paid the property taxes, attaching the certification. The credit is refundable, so any amount above your income tax is refunded.

### Can a surviving spouse claim the Wisconsin credit?

Yes, in many cases. An unremarried surviving spouse of an eligible veteran, or of a service member who died while on active duty, may claim the credit. Confirm the conditions with the Wisconsin Department of Veterans Affairs.

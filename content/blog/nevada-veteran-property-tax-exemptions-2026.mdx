If you serve in the military or have served, Nevada can be a friendly state for your wallet. It has no state income tax, and it offers a property tax break that lowers the bill on a disabled veteran's home. Nevada draws many military families after a PCS, which is short for Permanent Change of Station, the official military move from one duty station to another, with Nellis Air Force Base and Creech Air Force Base anchoring the Las Vegas area and Naval Air Station Fallon in the north. This guide covers the main rules in plain language and points you to the state's own sources. Tax law changes, so treat these figures as a starting point and confirm the current rules before you file.

## How Nevada Helps Disabled Veterans With Property Taxes

Nevada does not waive a disabled veteran's whole property tax bill. Instead, it offers a Disabled Veteran's Exemption that lowers the taxable value of what you own. As the [Nevada Department of Veterans Services explains](https://veterans.nv.gov/real-property-vehicle-tax-exemptions/), the exemption is for veterans who have a permanent service-connected disability of at least 60 percent, and the amount depends on the degree of disability. The U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, known as the VA, is the federal agency that rates service-connected disabilities.

### How the Exemption Amounts Work

Nevada states every property tax exemption as an amount of "assessed value." Assessed value is about 35 percent of a property's taxable value, so the exemption lowers the part of your home's value that the county actually taxes. According to the [Clark County Assessor](https://www.clarkcountynv.gov/government/assessor/exemption), the Disabled Veteran's Exemption amounts for the current fiscal year are:

- 60 to 79 percent disability rating: $17,700 of assessed value
- 80 to 99 percent disability rating: $26,550 of assessed value
- 100 percent disability rating: $35,400 of assessed value

These amounts are set by Nevada law and adjust every year for inflation, so the exact figure rises over time. Because the break lowers your assessed value rather than erasing the bill, the dollars you save depend on your local tax rate. A more modest home may have much of its tax covered, while a higher-value home still owes tax on the value above the exemption. Confirm the current amount with your county assessor before you count on it.

### Who Qualifies

To claim the Disabled Veteran's Exemption, you must be a Nevada resident with an honorable separation from service and a permanent service-connected disability rating of at least 60 percent from the VA. You show the county your discharge papers and your VA documentation of the disability percentage. There is no income limit on this exemption.

### Surviving Spouses

The benefit can carry to a surviving spouse. The Clark County Assessor notes that the Disabled Veteran's Exemption may pass to the surviving spouse of a disabled veteran who was eligible for the exemption at the time of death. Because the details depend on your situation, confirm them with your county assessor before you rely on the benefit.

## How It Works With Your Local Tax Bill

Property tax in Nevada is handled at the county level, and you claim your exemption through the county assessor where you live. One feature is unusual and useful: you can apply the exemption to your real property tax bill, or instead apply it to the Governmental Services Tax you pay when you register a vehicle with the Department of Motor Vehicles. Many veterans split the exemption between the two.

Nevada also offers a smaller Veteran's Exemption for wartime veterans who do not have a qualifying disability, which the Clark County Assessor lists at $3,540 of assessed value for the current year. The disabled veteran amounts above are larger and are the ones most service-connected veterans will use. ## Military Pay and Nevada's No Income Tax

Here is the part that makes Nevada stand out: it has no state personal income tax. As the [Nevada Department of Taxation](https://tax.nv.gov/) confirms, the state does not levy a tax on personal income at all. For service members and veterans, that is a real advantage.

Because Nevada does not tax personal income, it does not tax your active-duty military pay, your military retirement pay, or your VA disability compensation at the state level. You will still owe federal taxes, but you can leave the state income tax line off your Nevada budget. That makes claiming Nevada as your home state simple and favorable while you are stationed here.

## A Note for Military Spouses: MSRRA

If you are a military spouse, the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act, known as MSRRA, may still matter to you. MSRRA is a federal law that lets a military spouse keep a home state for tax and voting purposes even after moving on military orders. You do not automatically become a Nevada resident just because your service member got orders here.

Since Nevada has no state income tax, claiming Nevada as your residence can be simple and favorable while you are stationed here. Residency rules can get detailed, especially if you still earn income tied to another state, so confirm how MSRRA applies to your family before you make a change. For the property tax exemption in this guide, what usually matters most is that you are a Nevada resident who owns and lives in the home.

## How to Apply and Where to Verify

You apply for the Disabled Veteran's Exemption through your county assessor, with a valid Nevada driver's license or ID, your discharge papers, and VA documentation of your disability percentage. After your first filing, the assessor mails a renewal card each year to continue the exemption. Because the exemption amounts adjust every year for inflation, confirm the current figure with your county assessor and the [Nevada Department of Veterans Services](https://veterans.nv.gov/real-property-vehicle-tax-exemptions/) before you file.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do disabled veterans pay property taxes in Nevada?

Often a reduced amount. Nevada's Disabled Veteran's Exemption lowers your home's assessed value, by $17,700 to $35,400 depending on your disability rating for the current year. It is not a full waiver, so a higher-value home still owes tax on the value above the exemption. Confirm the current amount with your county assessor and the [Nevada Department of Veterans Services](https://veterans.nv.gov/real-property-vehicle-tax-exemptions/).

### How much is the Nevada Disabled Veteran's Exemption?

For the current fiscal year, the Clark County Assessor lists $17,700 of assessed value for a 60 to 79 percent rating, $26,550 for 80 to 99 percent, and $35,400 for 100 percent. The amounts are set by state law and adjust each year for inflation.

### Does Nevada tax military retirement pay?

No. Nevada has no state personal income tax, so it does not tax active-duty pay, military retirement pay, or VA disability compensation at the state level. You will still owe federal taxes.

### Can I use my veteran exemption on a vehicle instead of my home?

Yes. Nevada lets you apply the exemption to your real property tax bill or to the Governmental Services Tax when you register a vehicle with the DMV, and you can split it between the two. Your county assessor can walk you through it.

### Can a surviving spouse keep the Nevada exemption?

In many cases, yes. The Disabled Veteran's Exemption can pass to the surviving spouse of a disabled veteran who was eligible at the time of death. Confirm the conditions with your county assessor.

Property tax rules and dollar amounts change from year to year, and your situation may have details a general guide cannot cover.

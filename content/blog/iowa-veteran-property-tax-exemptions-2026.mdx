If you serve in the military or have served, Iowa offers one of the clearest property tax breaks in the country for fully disabled veterans, along with income tax rules that protect military pay. Iowa is home to Camp Dodge, the Iowa National Guard's main training site in Johnston, and the Des Moines Air National Guard base at the Des Moines airport, so it draws many military families after a PCS, which is short for Permanent Change of Station, the official military move from one duty station to another. This guide explains the main rules in plain language and points you to the state's own sources. Tax law changes, so treat these figures as a starting point and confirm the current rules before you file.

## How Iowa Helps Disabled Veterans With Property Taxes

Iowa gives a full property tax credit to its most severely disabled veterans. The U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, known as the VA, is the federal agency that rates service-connected disabilities, and the highest rating opens the door to this benefit.

As the [Iowa Department of Revenue explains](https://revenue.iowa.gov/taxes/tax-guidance/tax-credits-deductions-exemption/disabled-veteran-homestead-property-tax-credit), the Disabled Veteran Homestead Property Tax Credit equals 100 percent of the actual tax levy on the homestead. For a qualifying veteran who owns and lives in the home, this credit can offset the entire property tax bill on the residence.

### Who Qualifies

To qualify, you must be a veteran with a permanent service-connected disability rating of 100 percent, or a veteran with a permanent and total disability rating based on individual unemployability who is paid at the 100 percent disability rate. You must own and occupy the property as a homestead on July 1, declare Iowa as your residence for income tax purposes, and occupy the home for at least six months each year. Service members and those in nursing homes who do not occupy the home can still qualify.

### How Much the Credit Is Worth and What It Covers

The credit covers 100 percent of the property tax on the homestead. There are size limits on the land: up to one-half acre when the home and outbuildings are within a city, and up to 40 acres when the property is outside city limits. Because it is a full credit on the homestead rather than a partial reduction, a qualifying veteran's residence can be relieved of property tax entirely within those limits.

### Surviving Spouses

The benefit can carry to a surviving spouse. The surviving spouse of a qualifying disabled veteran who is receiving VA dependency and indemnity compensation may also receive the credit. Confirm the conditions with your county or city assessor and the Iowa Department of Veterans Affairs.

## How It Works With Your Local Tax Bill

Property tax in Iowa is assessed and collected at the local level, and you claim this credit through your county or city assessor. You file the application on or before July 1, including a VA Benefit Summary Letter showing your qualifying rating and a copy of your DD-214. Once the credit is allowed, you generally do not need to refile each year as long as you keep owning and occupying the home and your eligibility does not change.

Iowa also offers a separate, smaller military service tax exemption that many veterans receive on their property, which is different from the full disabled veteran credit described here. ## Military Pay and Iowa State Income Tax

Iowa moved to a flat individual income tax of 3.8 percent beginning with the 2025 tax year, and it shields military income broadly. The way the state handles your pay depends on the type of pay, but the result for most military families is little or no tax on service income.

Active-duty pay is not taxed. As the state's [military tax guidance](https://revenue.iowa.gov/taxes/tax-guidance/individual-income-tax/military-tax-information) explains, pay received from the federal government for active military service, including active duty in the Guard or Reserve, is excluded from Iowa income tax. Military retirement pay is also fully excluded, a benefit Iowa has provided since the 2014 tax year regardless of the retiree's age, and the exclusion reaches military survivor benefits as well. VA disability compensation is not part of your federal income, so Iowa does not tax it either. Between excluded active-duty pay, excluded retirement pay, and untaxed VA compensation, many military families owe little or no Iowa income tax on their service income. Because rates and rules can change, confirm the current details with the Department of Revenue before you file.

## A Note for Military Spouses: MSRRA

If you are a military spouse, the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act, known as MSRRA, may matter to you. MSRRA is a federal law that lets a military spouse keep a home state for tax and voting purposes even after moving on military orders. You do not automatically become an Iowa resident just because your service member got orders here.

Under the related federal rules, a service member, the spouse, or both may choose the service member's home state, the spouse's home state, or the service member's permanent duty station for residency. Because that choice affects both states' taxes, confirm yours before you file. For the property tax credit in this guide, what usually matters most is that the qualifying veteran owns and occupies the home as the homestead and declares Iowa residency.

## How to Apply and Where to Verify

You apply for the Disabled Veteran Homestead Property Tax Credit through your county or city assessor on or before July 1, with a VA Benefit Summary Letter showing your 100 percent service-connected or individual-unemployability rating and a copy of your DD-214. Because the rules and deadlines can change, confirm the current details with your assessor, the [Iowa Department of Revenue](https://revenue.iowa.gov/taxes/tax-guidance/tax-credits-deductions-exemption/disabled-veteran-homestead-property-tax-credit), and the [Iowa Department of Veterans Affairs](https://va.iowa.gov/) before you file.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do disabled veterans pay property taxes in Iowa?

A qualifying veteran often does not. The Disabled Veteran Homestead Property Tax Credit equals 100 percent of the tax levy on the homestead, within the program's land-size limits, so a fully disabled veteran's residence can be relieved of property tax. Confirm your eligibility with your county or city assessor.

### What disability rating do I need for the Iowa veteran credit?

You need a permanent service-connected disability rating of 100 percent, or a permanent and total rating based on individual unemployability that is paid at the 100 percent disability rate. A surviving spouse receiving VA dependency and indemnity compensation may also qualify.

### Does Iowa tax military retirement pay?

No. Iowa fully excludes military retirement pay from state income tax, regardless of age, a benefit in place since the 2014 tax year, and the exclusion reaches military survivor benefits. Active-duty pay is also excluded, and VA disability compensation is not taxed.

### When do I apply for the Iowa disabled veteran credit?

You file the application with your county or city assessor on or before July 1, including a VA Benefit Summary Letter and a copy of your DD-214. Once allowed, you generally do not need to refile each year unless your ownership, occupancy, or eligibility changes.

### Can a surviving spouse claim the Iowa credit?

Yes, in many cases. The surviving spouse of a qualifying disabled veteran who receives VA dependency and indemnity compensation may receive the credit. Confirm the conditions with your county or city assessor and the Iowa Department of Veterans Affairs.

Property tax rules and dollar amounts change from year to year, and your situation may have details a general guide cannot cover.

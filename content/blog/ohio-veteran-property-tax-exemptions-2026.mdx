If you serve in the military or have served, an Ohio veteran property tax exemption can take a real bite out of your yearly housing cost. Ohio draws many military families after a PCS, which is short for Permanent Change of Station, the official military move from one duty station to another, with Wright-Patterson Air Force Base anchoring the Dayton area. This guide walks through Ohio's enhanced homestead exemption for disabled veterans in plain language and points you to the state's own sources. Tax law changes, so treat these figures as a starting point and confirm the current amounts before you file.

## The Ohio Enhanced Homestead Exemption for Disabled Veterans

Ohio's main property tax break for disabled veterans is a stronger version of the state's homestead exemption. The homestead exemption shields part of a home's value from taxation, which lowers the tax bill. For most senior or disabled homeowners, the standard exemption shields the first $29,000 of the home's appraised value and comes with an income limit. Disabled veterans get a larger break with no income test.

Under the enhanced homestead exemption, an eligible disabled veteran can shield the first $58,000 of the home's appraised value from property tax, as the [Franklin County Auditor explains](https://auditor.franklincountyohio.gov/Real-Estate/Homestead). For example, an eligible owner of a home appraised at $200,000 would be taxed as if it were worth $142,000. The exact dollar savings depend on your local tax rate, and the state adjusts this amount for inflation over time, so confirm the current figure with your county auditor.

### Who Qualifies

To receive the enhanced exemption, the [Ohio disabled veteran homestead application (Form DTE 105I)](https://tax.ohio.gov/static/forms/real_property/DTE_105I.pdf) lays out the rules. You must be a veteran who was discharged or released under honorable conditions and who has received from the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, known as the VA, either a total disability rating of 100 percent or a 100 percent rating for compensation based on individual unemployability. Individual unemployability, often shortened to IU, means the VA pays you at the 100 percent level because service-connected disabilities keep you from holding steady work, even if your combined rating is below 100 percent.

One detail works strongly in veterans' favor. Unlike the standard homestead exemption for seniors and other disabled homeowners, the enhanced exemption for disabled veterans has no income limit. Your income does not reduce or block the benefit.

### Surviving Spouses

The benefit can carry over to a surviving spouse. If a veteran was receiving the enhanced exemption at the time of death, the surviving spouse can keep it as long as they own and occupy the home and do not remarry. Ohio also extends the same enhanced exemption to surviving spouses of public service officers, such as police and firefighters, who were killed in the line of duty.

## How It Works With Local Property Taxes

Property tax in Ohio is billed and collected at the county level, and the homestead exemption is processed by your county auditor. The exemption shows up as a credit on your tax bill rather than a separate check. That is why two veterans with the same rating can see different dollar savings depending on where they live and what their local tax rate is.

The enhanced veteran exemption is the main property tax break tied to your home, and you generally claim one homestead exemption per principal residence. ## Military Pay and Ohio State Income Tax

Ohio does have a state income tax, so how your military income is treated matters. The good news is that Ohio shields the income most service members and retirees rely on.

Ohio lets you deduct your military retirement pay. As the [Ohio Department of Taxation explains in its military tax guidance](https://tax.ohio.gov/help-center/resources/tax-education/going-into-military), taxpayers who retired from the active or reserve components of the U.S. Army, Navy, Air Force, Marine Corps, Coast Guard, or National Guard can deduct the retired pay they receive, to the extent it is included in their federal adjusted gross income. In plain terms, Ohio does not tax your military pension.

Active-duty pay gets a break too. The same guidance allows a deduction for military pay earned while a service member is stationed outside Ohio on permanent active duty. VA disability compensation is not part of your federal income to begin with, so Ohio does not tax it either. Ohio has also been lowering and flattening its income tax rates in recent years, so check the current rates and deduction rules with the Department of Taxation before you file.

## A Note for Military Spouses: MSRRA

If you are a military spouse, the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act, known as MSRRA, may matter to you. MSRRA is a federal law that lets a military spouse keep a home state for tax and voting purposes even after moving on military orders. You do not automatically become an Ohio resident just because your service member got orders here.

Under the related federal rules, a service member, their spouse, or both may choose the service member's home state, the spouse's home state, or the service member's permanent duty station for residency. Because that choice affects both states' taxes, confirm yours before you file. For the property tax exemption in this guide, what usually matters most is whether the home is the veteran's principal residence.

## How to Apply and Where to Verify

You apply for the enhanced homestead exemption through your county auditor, not the state. You file [Form DTE 105I, the Homestead Exemption Application for Disabled Veterans](https://tax.ohio.gov/static/forms/real_property/DTE_105I.pdf), and attach a copy of your DD214 discharge document plus your VA award letter showing the 100 percent rating or the individual unemployability determination. The form is due to the county auditor on or before December 31, and Ohio allows a late application for the prior year on the same property.

Because the exempted amount is adjusted for inflation and local tax rates vary, ask your county auditor for the current figures. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do 100 percent disabled veterans pay property taxes in Ohio?

Ohio does not erase the bill, but it offers an enhanced homestead exemption that shields the first $58,000 of a qualifying disabled veteran's home value from tax, with no income limit. A veteran with a 100 percent service-connected rating or a 100 percent rating for individual unemployability can claim it. Confirm your eligibility with your county auditor and the [Ohio disabled veteran homestead form](https://tax.ohio.gov/static/forms/real_property/DTE_105I.pdf).

### How much is the Ohio enhanced homestead exemption for veterans?

It shields the first $58,000 of your home's appraised value from property tax, compared with $29,000 for the standard homestead exemption. The dollar savings depend on your local tax rate, and the amount is adjusted for inflation, so verify the current figure with your county auditor.

### Does Ohio tax military retirement pay?

No. Ohio lets you deduct military retirement pay to the extent it is included in your federal adjusted gross income, so your military pension is effectively not taxed by the state. Active-duty pay earned while stationed outside Ohio is also deductible.

### Is there an income limit for the Ohio veteran homestead exemption?

No. Unlike the standard homestead exemption for seniors and other disabled homeowners, which has an income limit, the enhanced exemption for disabled veterans has no income test.

### Can a surviving spouse keep the veteran's exemption?

In many cases, yes. If the veteran was receiving the enhanced exemption at the time of death, the surviving spouse can keep it as long as they own and occupy the home and do not remarry.

Property tax rules and dollar amounts change from year to year, and your situation may have details a general guide cannot cover.

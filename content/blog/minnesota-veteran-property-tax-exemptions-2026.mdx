If you serve in the military or have served, it helps to know how Minnesota handles property taxes for veterans. Minnesota is home to Camp Ripley, the Minnesota National Guard's main training center near Little Falls, along with Air National Guard and Reserve units around Minneapolis and St. Paul, so it draws many military families after a PCS, which is short for Permanent Change of Station, the official military move from one duty station to another. Minnesota does not erase a disabled veteran's property tax bill. Instead, it removes a large slice of the home's value from taxation through a program that scales with your VA rating. This guide explains how that works in plain language and points you to the state's own sources. Tax law changes, so treat these figures as a starting point and confirm the current rules before you file.

## How Minnesota Helps Disabled Veterans With Property Taxes

Minnesota lowers a disabled veteran's property tax by excluding part of the home's market value before the tax is calculated. The U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, known as the VA, is the federal agency that rates service-connected disabilities, and the size of that rating sets the size of the exclusion.

As the [Minnesota Department of Revenue explains](https://www.revenue.state.mn.us/market-value-exclusion-veterans-disability), the Market Value Exclusion for Veterans with a Disability reduces the market value of the home for tax purposes, which can reduce the property tax. The program is administered by the counties.

### Who Qualifies

To qualify, you must have been honorably discharged from the U.S. armed forces, shown by a DD-214 or other official discharge papers, and you must be certified by the VA as having a service-connected disability with a rating of 70 percent or higher. You must also own and occupy the home as your homestead. The exclusion is built for veterans with a significant service-connected disability, so a rating of at least 70 percent is the key requirement.

### How Much the Exclusion Is Worth

The amount of value removed from taxation depends on your rating. A veteran the VA has rated as totally and permanently disabled at the 100 percent level can exclude $300,000 of the home's market value. A veteran with a service-connected rating of 70 percent or higher, but not totally and permanently disabled, can exclude $150,000 of the home's market value. Because the exclusion comes off the value before the tax is figured, it lowers the bill rather than wiping it out for a higher-value home.

### Surviving Spouses

The benefit can carry to a surviving spouse. The surviving spouse of a veteran who held the 100 percent total and permanent rating may continue the exclusion, and the spouse may also qualify if the veteran died from a service-connected cause while in active service or if the spouse receives VA dependency and indemnity compensation. A surviving spouse generally reapplies by the annual deadline. Confirm the conditions with your county assessor.

## How It Works With Your Local Tax Bill

Property tax in Minnesota is assessed and collected at the local level, and your county administers this exclusion. You apply through your county assessor with your VA documentation showing the qualifying rating, and the application is generally due by December 15 to take effect for the next year's taxes. Some counties use a slightly different date, so check locally.

Once the exclusion is in place, you usually do not need to reapply each year as the disabled veteran, though a surviving spouse does reapply. ## Military Pay and Minnesota State Income Tax

Minnesota has a graduated income tax with rates that reach into the higher single digits, but it shields military income broadly through two subtractions. The way the state handles your pay depends on the type of pay, but the result for many military families is little or no tax on service income.

Active-duty pay is covered by a subtraction. As the state's [military pay subtraction guidance](https://www.revenue.state.mn.us/military-pay-subtraction) explains, a Minnesota resident in the U.S. or United Nations armed forces may subtract federally taxable active-duty military pay on Schedule M1M. Military retirement pay is covered by a separate subtraction. The [military pension subtraction](https://www.revenue.state.mn.us/military-pension-subtraction) lets you subtract retirement pay for service in the active or reserve component, also on Schedule M1M, though you cannot claim it in the same year you claim the nonrefundable credit for past military service. VA disability compensation is not part of your federal income, so Minnesota does not tax it either. Because rates and rules can change, confirm the current details with the Department of Revenue before you file.

## A Note for Military Spouses: MSRRA

If you are a military spouse, the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act, known as MSRRA, may matter to you. MSRRA is a federal law that lets a military spouse keep a home state for tax and voting purposes even after moving on military orders. You do not automatically become a Minnesota resident just because your service member got orders here.

Under the related federal rules, a service member, the spouse, or both may choose the service member's home state, the spouse's home state, or the service member's permanent duty station for residency. Because that choice affects both states' taxes, confirm yours before you file. For the property tax exclusion in this guide, what usually matters most is that the qualifying veteran owns and occupies the home as the homestead.

## How to Apply and Where to Verify

You apply for the Market Value Exclusion for Veterans with a Disability through your county assessor, generally by December 15, with a VA letter confirming your service-connected rating of 70 percent or higher. Because the rules and deadlines can vary by county, confirm the current details with your county assessor, the [Minnesota Department of Revenue](https://www.revenue.state.mn.us/market-value-exclusion-veterans-disability), and the [Minnesota Department of Veterans Affairs](https://mn.gov/mdva/) before you file.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do disabled veterans pay property taxes in Minnesota?

Often a reduced amount. Minnesota excludes part of a qualifying veteran's home value from taxation, $300,000 for a 100 percent total and permanent rating and $150,000 for a service-connected rating of 70 percent or higher. It lowers the taxable value rather than erasing the bill, so a higher-value home still owes tax on the value above the exclusion. Confirm your eligibility with your county assessor.

### What disability rating do I need for the Minnesota veteran exclusion?

You need a VA-certified service-connected disability of 70 percent or higher. A rating of 70 to 99 percent allows a $150,000 market value exclusion, and a total and permanent rating at the 100 percent level allows a $300,000 exclusion.

### Does Minnesota tax military retirement pay?

Generally no. Minnesota allows a subtraction for military retirement pay on Schedule M1M, and a separate subtraction for active-duty pay. You cannot claim the retirement subtraction in the same year as the nonrefundable credit for past military service. VA disability compensation is not taxed.

### When do I apply for the Minnesota veteran exclusion?

You apply through your county assessor, generally by December 15, to have the exclusion apply to the next year's taxes. Bring your VA documentation showing the qualifying rating, and check your county for its exact deadline.

### Can a surviving spouse claim the Minnesota exclusion?

Yes, in many cases. The surviving spouse of a veteran who held the 100 percent total and permanent rating may continue the exclusion, and a spouse may qualify if the veteran died from a service-connected cause in active service or if the spouse receives dependency and indemnity compensation. A surviving spouse reapplies by the annual deadline. Confirm the conditions with your county assessor.

Property tax rules and dollar amounts change from year to year, and your situation may have details a general guide cannot cover.

Planning your next PCS move and considering a VA loan assumption? There's a critical entitlement rule that could make or break your homebuying strategy—and most military families don't know about it until it's too late.

VA loan assumptions offer incredible potential savings in today's interest rate environment. If you can assume a loan with a 2021-era rate of 2.75% instead of securing a new loan at current rates, you could save hundreds per month and tens of thousands over the loan's lifetime. But this powerful benefit comes with strict requirements that aren't always clearly explained.

According to [VA.gov](https://www.va.gov/housing-assistance/home-loans/loan-types/purchase-loan/), VA loan assumptions allow qualified buyers to take over an existing VA-backed mortgage, including its original interest rate and terms. However, the entitlement requirements are more complex than many realize.

## **How VA Loan Assumptions Work**

A VA loan assumption transfers the existing mortgage from seller to buyer, preserving the original loan terms. This process offers several advantages:

**Interest Rate Preservation:** You inherit the seller's original rate, which may be significantly lower than current market rates.

**Reduced Closing Costs:** Assumptions typically involve lower fees than originating a new loan.

**Faster Processing:** The assumption process can be quicker than traditional mortgage approval, beneficial for tight PCS timelines.

The [Department of Veterans Affairs](https://www.va.gov/housing-assistance/home-loans/loan-types/purchase-loan/) requires all VA loan assumptions to be formally approved, ensuring the new buyer meets creditworthiness standards.

## **The Critical Entitlement Rule Most People Miss**

Here's where many military homebuyers—and even some lenders—make a costly mistake:

**You must have enough remaining entitlement to cover the original loan amount, not just the current balance.**

Let's illustrate with a real scenario: You're a service member who previously used your VA loan and have $360,000 in remaining entitlement. You find a home where the seller owes $350,000 on their VA loan. At first glance, you have sufficient entitlement.

However, if that seller originally purchased the home for $400,000 using their VA loan, you need $400,000 in entitlement to assume it—regardless of the current $350,000 balance.

This distinction catches many military families off-guard, especially those who've used their VA benefit before and are working with their remaining "bonus entitlement."

## **Understanding Your VA Loan Entitlement**

[VA.gov](https://www.va.gov/housing-assistance/home-loans/how-to-apply/) explains that most eligible service members have a basic entitlement of $36,000, plus additional entitlement that varies by county loan limits. For 2025, this typically provides $766,550 in total loan guaranty for most areas.

If you've used your VA loan previously, your remaining entitlement equals your total available entitlement minus the amount tied to your existing VA loan. This calculation becomes crucial for assumptions.

To check your remaining entitlement:

- Log into the [VA's eBenefits portal](https://www.ebenefits.va.gov/)
- Review your Certificate of Eligibility (COE)
- Consult with a VA-approved lender for detailed calculations

## **What Happens When Entitlement Falls Short**

Insufficient entitlement doesn't automatically disqualify you from a VA loan assumption, but it creates additional requirements:

**Cash Payment Required:** You may need to pay the difference between your available entitlement and the required amount in cash at closing.

**Alternative Financing:** Some buyers combine a partial assumption with additional financing, though this complicates the transaction.

**Assumption Denial:** In some cases, insufficient entitlement results in assumption rejection, requiring you to pursue traditional financing instead.

According to [Military OneSource](https://www.militaryonesource.mil/money-credit-debt/), these scenarios particularly impact military families during PCS moves when cash reserves may be limited.

## **Seller Considerations: Protecting Your VA Loan Benefits**

Military homeowners selling properties with VA loans face their own considerations regarding assumptions:

**Entitlement Restoration:** When a buyer assumes your VA loan, your entitlement remains tied to that property until the loan is fully satisfied or the buyer substitutes their own entitlement.

**Timeline Impact:** Entitlement restoration isn't immediate. The [VA's loan servicer guidelines](https://www.va.gov/housing-assistance/home-loans/) indicate restoration processing can take several weeks, potentially affecting your ability to use VA benefits for your next home purchase.

**Substitution Benefits:** If the assuming buyer is VA-eligible and substitutes their entitlement, you regain immediate access to your full VA loan benefit.

Military families planning back-to-back home purchases should factor these timelines into their PCS planning.

## **Critical Questions Before Pursuing a VA Loan Assumption**

**For Military Buyers:**

- What is my current remaining VA loan entitlement?
- What was the seller's original loan amount (not current balance)?
- Does the seller's loan meet VA assumption requirements?
- Can I meet the lender's credit and income standards for assumption approval?

**For Military Sellers:**

- Do I need my VA entitlement restored for my next home purchase?
- Will the buyer substitute their own VA entitlement?
- How will the assumption timeline affect my PCS plans?
- Should I consider conventional sale instead of assumption?

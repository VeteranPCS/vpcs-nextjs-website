If you serve in the military or have served, an Arizona veteran property tax exemption can lower what you owe on your home, and for some veterans it can erase the bill on a primary residence entirely. Arizona is a popular landing spot after a PCS, which is short for Permanent Change of Station, the official military move from one duty station to another, and it has grown more generous to veterans in the last few years. This guide walks through the main rules in plain language and points you to the state's own sources. Tax law changes, so treat these figures as a starting point and confirm the current amounts before you file.

## The Arizona Disabled Veteran Property Tax Exemption

Arizona offers a property tax exemption to honorably discharged veterans who have a service-connected or nonservice-connected disability. The VA is the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, the federal agency that rates service-connected disabilities. This exemption is set in Arizona law under [A.R.S. Section 42-11111](https://www.azleg.gov/viewdocument/?docName=https://www.azleg.gov/ars/42/11111.htm) and is run by the county assessor in the county where your home sits.

Arizona recently rewrote how this exemption works. According to the [Arizona Department of Revenue property tax FAQs](https://azdor.gov/business/property-tax/property-tax-faqs), the updated rules apply to tax years that begin on or after January 1, 2026, and they sort veterans into three groups:

- Service-connected with a 100 percent VA disability rating: a full exemption of the qualifying veteran's primary residence.
- Service-connected with less than a 100 percent rating: an exemption up to a maximum dollar amount, prorated to match your VA rating.
- Nonservice-connected disability at any rating: an exemption up to a maximum dollar amount, prorated to match your VA rating.

In short, a veteran with a 100 percent service-connected rating can take the full value of a primary home off the tax rolls, while veterans with a lower or nonservice-connected rating get a partial break that scales with their rating.

### Income Limit and the Old Value Cap

Two limits used to shrink this benefit. The Department of Revenue says the 2026 update removed the assessed property value cap for the veteran exemptions, so a higher-value home no longer disqualifies a veteran. A household income limit still applies to every individual exemption under the statute, including the veteran exemption. The income ceiling and the maximum partial-exemption amount are set dollar figures that Arizona adjusts each year for inflation, so confirm the current numbers with your county assessor before counting on them.

### Surviving Spouses

The full exemption can follow a surviving spouse. If a veteran held the 100 percent service-connected full exemption on the home, the unremarried surviving spouse can keep that exemption on the same residence, as the Department of Revenue's FAQs explain. The benefit stays with the property, so it ends if the home is sold.

## How It Works With Local Property Taxes

Arizona has no statewide property tax. Taxes are levied locally by counties, cities, school districts, and special districts, and the county assessor administers exemptions. That is why two veterans with the same rating can see different dollar savings depending on where they live and what the local tax rate is.

Arizona does not have a broad homestead exemption that every homeowner can claim, the way some states do. The veteran exemption above is the main property tax break tied to your home, and you may only claim one individual exemption even if you qualify under more than one category. ## Military Pay and Arizona State Income Tax

Arizona does have a state income tax, so how your military income is treated matters. The good news is that Arizona shields most military pay.

According to the [Arizona Department of Revenue's military tax filing page](https://azdor.gov/individuals/income-tax-filing-assistance/military-tax-filing), Arizona has not taxed active-duty military pay since 2006, and members can subtract that pay to the extent it is included in Arizona income. Pay for active service as a National Guard member or reservist is also subtracted.

Military retirement is treated just as well. The same page states that Arizona excludes benefits, annuities, and pensions paid as retired or retainer pay of the uniformed services for tax year 2021 and forward. That means your military retirement pay is fully exempt from Arizona income tax. VA disability compensation is not part of your federal income to begin with, so Arizona does not tax it either. For a broader rundown of state benefits, see the [Arizona Department of Veterans' Services benefits guide](https://dvs.az.gov/sites/default/files/2025-07/Arizona%20State%20Veteran%20Benefits%20Guide%20(2025%20Edit).pdf).

## A Note for Military Spouses: MSRRA

If you are a military spouse, the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act, known as MSRRA, may matter to you. MSRRA is a federal law that lets a military spouse keep a home state for tax and voting purposes even after moving on military orders. You do not automatically become an Arizona resident just because your service member got orders here.

Arizona follows the current federal rules. As the Department of Revenue's military page notes, for tax year 2023 and beyond, a service member and spouse may each elect to use the residence or domicile of the member, the residence or domicile of the spouse, or the member's permanent duty station for tax purposes. Because that choice affects both states' taxes, confirm yours before you file. For the property tax exemption in this guide, what usually matters most is whether the home is the veteran's primary residence.

## How to Apply and Where to Verify

You apply for the disabled veteran property tax exemption through your county assessor, not the state. The Department of Revenue's FAQs say new applications are filed during the annual window that runs from the first Monday in January through March 1. If a veteran already received the exemption before January 1, 2026, no new application is needed to get the benefit of the updated rules. You will need your VA paperwork showing your disability rating, plus proof the home is your primary residence.

Because the maximum partial-exemption amount and the income limit are adjusted every year, ask your county assessor for the current figures rather than relying on last year's numbers. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do 100 percent disabled veterans pay property taxes in Arizona?

For tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2026, a veteran with a 100 percent service-connected VA disability rating can receive a full exemption on their primary residence, which can bring the property tax on that home to zero. A household income limit still applies, so confirm your eligibility with your county assessor and the [Arizona Department of Revenue](https://azdor.gov/business/property-tax/property-tax-faqs).

### How much is the Arizona disabled veteran exemption for a lower rating?

Veterans with a service-connected rating below 100 percent, or with a nonservice-connected disability, get an exemption up to a maximum dollar amount that is prorated to match their VA rating. That maximum is set by the state and adjusted each year, so ask your county assessor for the current amount.

### Does Arizona tax military retirement pay?

No. Arizona excludes uniformed services retired or retainer pay for tax year 2021 and forward, so military retirement pay is fully exempt from Arizona income tax. Active-duty pay has not been taxed since 2006.

### Can a surviving spouse keep the veteran's property tax exemption?

In many cases, yes. The unremarried surviving spouse of a veteran who held the 100 percent service-connected full exemption can keep that exemption on the same home. The benefit is tied to the property and ends if the home is sold.

### Is there an income limit for the Arizona veteran exemption?

Yes. A household income limit applies to the veteran exemption, even after the 2026 update removed the assessed-value cap for veterans. The limit is adjusted yearly, so verify the current figure with your county assessor before you apply.
